War of the Worlds (1938)
In 1898, H.G. Wells published The War of the Worlds. 40 years later, the novel was adapted as a Halloween episode of the drama series The Mercury Theatre on the Air on CBS radio, directed and narrated by Orson Welles. The Mercury Theatre writers changed the setting of the episode from locations in Europe to towns and cities on the east coast of the United States, but the original plot of an attack on Earth by an army from Mars remained the same. It is said to have caused mass panic across the country.
The episode begins as a casual music broadcast. However, after news of explosions on Mars and a meteorite landing in Grover's Mill, New Jersey, the music is cut short and a reporter goes to investigate. The meteorite is revealed to be a spaceship, and Martians emerge, using a heat ray to kill police and onlookers as well as set a nearby forest ablaze. Bulletins come in from up and down the mid-Atlantic states about similar sightings, and are read by the announcer in between eyewitness accounts and speeches by various military and government officials concerning the attacks. Earthly weapons seem to hardly do damage to the giant Martian machines. The frenzy culminates with another reporter broadcasting from the top of a studio in Manhattan as the Martians storm the city and release a cloud of poison gas. He narrates the gas's approach, coughs, and then seemingly collapses, dead.
At the end of the show, Welles goes out of character to state that it is fiction. "That was no Martian," he says, "it's Halloween." The broadcast also begins by announcing the Mercury Theatre and their rendition of Wells's novel. However, the listeners that tuned in for the middle of the show (during breaks in other, more popular shows on at the same time) took it as fact. The alarm was significant enough to send policemen to the radio studio to stop the broadcast, and a struggle ensued as CBS employees attempted to stop the police from breaking into the recording booth. News reporters, photographers, and more police were outside the building, so once they were off the air, the cast left through the building's back door.
Despite the thousands of news artibles written about the broadcast and resulting panic, many speculate that its effect was not as widespread as the headlines made it seem. Regardless, it demonstrated the effect radio could have on people. For more information, the Wikipedia page is a good place to start, as well as listening to the original broadcast .
